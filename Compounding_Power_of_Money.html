The Basic Mechanism

This section explains the key difference between simple interest and compound interest.

Simple vs Compound Interest

Simple Interest: Growth is calculated only on the original amount (Principal) you deposited. It grows in a straight line.

Compound Interest: Growth is calculated on both the principal and the previously earned interest. Interest earns interest, resulting in exponential growth over time.

The Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

A = Total amount, P = Principal, r = Annual interest rate, n = Compounding frequency, t = Time (years)

The Price of Time

Time is the most important factor in investing. Here we show how starting small early beats investing more later.

A Tale of Two Investors

Let's compare two investors who retire at age 65 with an annual return of 10%.

Investor 'A' — The Early Starter

Invests Rs. 10,000/month from age 25 to 35 only (just 10 years), then stops investing. But lets the money grow with compound interest until age 65.

Investor 'B' — The Late Starter

Waits until age 35 to start. Continuously invests Rs. 10,000/month from age 35 to 65 (30 full years).

Even though Investor B puts in three times the money, Investor A's fund grows larger — because of the extra time gained by starting early.

Rule of 72

A quick way to estimate how long it takes to double your investment. Simply divide 72 by your annual interest rate.

72 / 8% = 9 years
